1. Introduction

Given observed samples x\bold{x}x﻿ from a distribution of interest, the goal of a generative model is to learn to model its true data distribution p(x)p(\bold{x})p(x)﻿. This learned model can be used to generate new samples or evaluate the likelihood of observed/sampled data.

We usually think of the data we observed as represented or generated by an associated unseen latent variable, which can be denoted by random variable z\bold{z}z﻿.

3. Variational AutoEncoder (VAE)

In the default VAE, we directly maximize the ELBO.

It is ‘variational’ because we optimize for the best qϕ(z∣x)q_{\phi}(\bold{z} | \bold{x})qϕ​(z∣x)﻿ among a family of potential posterior distributions parameterized by ϕ\phiϕ﻿.

It is an ‘autoencoder’ because the input data is trained to predict itself after undergoing an intermediate bottlenecking representation step.

Now, we dissect the ELBO term further:

We learn an intermediate bottlenecking distribution qϕ(z∣x)q_{\phi} (\bold{z} | \bold{x})qϕ​(z∣x)﻿ that can be treated as an encoder. We also learn a deterministic function pθ(x∣z)p_{\theta}(\bold{x} | \bold{z})pθ​(x∣z)﻿ to convert a given latent vector z\bold{z}z﻿ into an observation x\bold{x}x﻿, which can be interpreted as a decoder.

The first term measures the reconstruction likelihood of the decoder from our variational distribution. The second term measures how similar the learned variational distribution is to a prior belief held over latent variables. Maximizing ELBO is thus equivalent to maximizing its first term and minimizing the second term.
